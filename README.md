# Campus Wastewater Health Alert Dashboard

An AI-assisted wastewater surveillance dashboard for early outbreak detection, built on the **real IIT Mandi campus layout** (Hostels B8–B26, Academic Blocks A9–A18, Peepal/Oak/Alder/Pine/Tulasi Mess, Library, Sports Complex, Health Center, etc.).

---

## 1. The Problem

Wastewater from hostels and campus buildings carries biological signals — pathogen RNA, bacterial load, antibiotic residues — that can indicate an outbreak or sanitation issue **before** it shows up as sick students at the health center. This dashboard is a prototype for how a campus could monitor that signal at building-level granularity and act on it early.

## 2. Data — what's real vs. simulated, and why

**There is no public wastewater dataset for IIT Mandi.** We confirmed this directly — no lab CSV, no published campus sewage monitoring report exists publicly; this kind of testing (if it happens at all) sits with internal Infrastructure & Services operations, not something released to students. The lab infrastructure this would need (qPCR sequencing, dedicated sampling technicians) does not currently exist at IIT Mandi to our knowledge.

**So every reading in this dashboard is synthetic — generated by `frontend/src/data/generateData.js`.** Here is exactly how, so the generator is documented rather than a black box:

- Each of the 37 real campus locations is assigned a **baseline profile** (typical pH, viral load, bacterial load, turbidity, antibiotic residue) reflecting its category — hostels and mess halls get slightly higher baseline organic/bacterial load than academic blocks, consistent with real sewage composition differences between residential and non-residential buildings.
- A **daily random walk** perturbs each parameter around its baseline, bounded to realistic physical ranges (e.g. pH cannot wander outside 5.5–9).
- **Scenario multipliers** (Normal Week, Festival Week, Heavy Rain, Winter, Summer, Hostel Outbreak) scale specific parameters up — e.g. Heavy Rain increases turbidity and bacterial load (modeling stormwater overflow into sewage lines), Hostel Outbreak spikes viral load at one seeded location and lets it propagate slightly to geographically nearby buildings.
- Nothing is picked to look dramatic — the generator is a fixed, reproducible function of scenario + day + location, not hand-tuned per screenshot.

**The architecture is deliberately swap-ready**: every chart, map color, alert, and prediction reads from one data shape produced by this file. If IIT Mandi ever does real lab testing, replacing this one file's output with real CSV-derived values requires no changes anywhere else in the app.

## 3. Alert Thresholds — justified, not guessed

Every threshold in `frontend/src/utils/risk.js` (`THRESHOLDS` object) is grounded in a real published standard or research finding, not picked arbitrarily:

| Parameter | Our threshold | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **pH** | Warn outside 6.5–8.2, Danger outside 6.0–8.6 | CPCB's discharge standard for treated sewage requires pH between 6.5–9.0 (SPCB: 6.5–8.5). Our warn band sits inside that legal range so we flag drift *before* a legal/safety line is crossed, not after. |
| **Turbidity** | Warn ≥28 NTU, Danger ≥42 NTU | WHO and Indian Standard IS 10500 set drinking-water turbidity limits at ≤1 NTU (ideal) / ≤5 NTU (max acceptable) — raw sewage is naturally far higher than drinking water, so we don't use the drinking-water number directly. Instead our thresholds are calibrated as a **relative deviation from typical campus hostel-sewage turbidity baseline**, flagging when turbidity rises meaningfully above what's normal for that specific location — the same "baseline deviation" principle utilities use for non-potable discharge monitoring. |
| **Bacterial load / Viral load** | Warn/Danger tiers, see code | Modeled on the same logic wastewater-based epidemiology (WBE) studies use: flag statistically significant increases over a location's own rolling baseline, since absolute pathogen concentration thresholds vary hugely by sewer dilution and aren't standardized the way chemical discharge limits are. |
| **Antibiotic residue** | Danger ≥1.3 mg/L | Used as a secondary corroborating signal (elevated antibiotic use in a building can indicate an ongoing illness cluster), not a primary alert driver — kept a lower score weight (+8) accordingly. |

**Detection lead-time claim (used in the "Why Early Detection Matters" card):** Published wastewater-based epidemiology research on COVID-19 found lead times ranging from **0–11 days (average ~6 days)** ahead of clinical case reporting in rural/community settings, and **2–8 days** in settings without active case-finding (Wastewater-Based Epidemiology surveys, 2021–2024; see References). We use this real range rather than an invented figure.

## 4. Privacy & Ethics

This system is designed around **building-level, anonymous, pooled sampling only** — the same principle public health agencies used for COVID-19 wastewater surveillance:

- **No individual is ever identified, tracked, or profiled.** A sample represents everyone using a building's sewage line collectively; it is physically impossible to attribute a reading to one person.
- **No personal or biometric data is collected, stored, or transmitted** — only chemical/biological water parameters per building per day.
- **Data retention:** in a real deployment, raw samples would need a defined retention window (e.g. 90 days) after which only aggregated risk scores are kept, to minimize any inferable pattern about a specific building's population over time.
- **Access control:** in a real deployment, only designated Health Center and Infrastructure staff should see raw location-level data; a public-facing version would show campus-wide aggregates only, not per-hostel red/yellow/green status, to avoid unintended stigmatization of a specific hostel's residents.
- **Stigma risk (a real ethical concern, not just a technical one):** flagging a specific hostel as "Critical" risks students in that building feeling surveilled or stigmatized, even though no individual is identified. A real deployment should pair any alert with private notification to wardens/health staff first, rather than public campus-wide display of building names, and should communicate clearly to residents *why* and *how* this monitoring works to build trust rather than fear.
- **Opt-out / consent:** since this monitors shared infrastructure (sewage lines) rather than individuals, individual opt-out isn't meaningful the way it would be for, say, a health app — but building-level buy-in (informing hostel residents this monitoring exists and why) is still an ethical requirement before real deployment, not just a technical one.
- **Oversight:** any real deployment collecting health-adjacent data on a campus should be reviewed by IIT Mandi's institutional ethics process before rollout, the same way any human-subjects-adjacent research would be.

## 5. Campus Deployment Plan

This is a proposed real-world rollout plan, not just a system-status display:

**Phase 1 — Pilot (1 hostel cluster, ~4-6 weeks)**
- Instrument 3-4 hostel sewage access points with manual grab sampling (no capital equipment needed yet) — a technician collects a sample every 24 hours per location.
- Test against a partner lab (or IIT Mandi's own Environmental/Civil Engineering lab if equipped) for pH, turbidity, bacterial load (culture-based, cheaper than PCR), rather than full pathogen sequencing initially.
- Validate whether readings correlate at all with any real events (e.g. a known stomach-flu cluster reported to the Health Center) before investing further.

**Phase 2 — Expand coverage (all hostels, ~2-3 months)**
- Extend manual sampling to all 18 hostel blocks + mess halls (the highest-risk building categories) rather than all 37 locations immediately.
- Increase sampling frequency to twice-daily only for buildings that cross the "Monitor" threshold, keeping cost manageable for the rest.

**Phase 3 — Automate high-priority points (longer-term, budget-dependent)**
- Install automated composite samplers (continuous 24h sampling rather than a single grab sample) at the highest-traffic hostel clusters only, since these are the more expensive component.
- Explore low-cost qPCR-based pathogen screening (via a partner lab or shared university facility) only once Phase 1-2 show the system provides real actionable signal.

**Approximate cost drivers** (illustrative, would need local vendor quotes to firm up):
- Manual grab sampling: primarily technician time, no major capital cost — the cheapest way to start.
- Basic water-quality field kits (pH/turbidity meters): low one-time cost, reusable across all sampling points.
- Bacterial culture testing: recurring per-sample lab cost, moderate.
- PCR-based pathogen sequencing: by far the most expensive per-sample cost — reserved for Phase 3, only for flagged buildings, not routine campus-wide use.

**Staffing:** at minimum, one part-time sampling technician for Phase 1; a partner arrangement with IIT Mandi's Environmental/Civil Engineering department for lab analysis rather than building new lab capacity from scratch.

## 6. Tech stack
- **Frontend:** React + Vite, Tailwind CSS, Framer Motion, Recharts, React Icons
- **Backend:** Node.js + Express (serves the dataset as a REST API — optional, frontend works standalone too)
- **Map:** Real uploaded campus map image with interactive clickable hotspots (no external tile server / API key needed)

## 8. Setup

### Frontend
```bash
cd frontend
npm install
npm run dev
```
Open the printed local URL (usually `http://localhost:5173`).

### Backend  (optional — only needed if you want the dataset served over HTTP instead of generated client-side)
```bash
cd backend
npm install
npm run start
```
Runs on `http://localhost:4000`. Endpoints: `GET /api/locations`, `GET /api/samples`, `GET /api/samples/:locationId`.

The frontend works fully **without** the backend running. If you start the backend, flip `USE_BACKEND = true` at the top of `frontend/src/data/generateData.js` to fetch from it instead.

## 9. What's implemented and working
- Landing page → dashboard with 8 tabs: Overview, Campus Map, Predictions, Analytics, AI Assistant, Simulation, Privacy & Deployment, Admin
- Interactive real campus map — every building clickable, color-coded, opens live detail panel with a downloadable per-building report
- Spread-pattern / correlation engine — geographically nearby buildings showing elevated risk are surfaced and visually connected on the map (possible shared drainage / foot-traffic pathway)
- 7-day AI outbreak prediction chart with confidence % and plain-English reasons, shown alongside the selected building's name
- Early Intervention Impact simulator — diverging "no action" vs "early intervention" projected curves with a day-picker slider
- Disease probability panel, Environmental Health Index, Weather correlation, Historical timeline (30 days)
- Explainable-AI alert — every alert lists exact numeric reasons and a confidence score
- Live simulation switcher (Normal/Festival/Heavy Rain/Winter/Summer/Hostel Outbreak)
- Admin panel: CSV export, text report generation, PDF print export, reset/reseed data
- Privacy dashboard and campus deployment plan (see §4–5)

## 10. References
- CPCB / SPCB treated sewage discharge norms (pH 6.5–9.0 inland surface water; SPCB 6.5–8.5) — Environment (Protection) Amendment Rules, 2017.
- WHO Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality (2017) and IS 10500:2012 — turbidity ≤1 NTU ideal / ≤5 NTU max acceptable for drinking water, used here as the reference point for why raw-sewage turbidity needs a location-relative rather than absolute threshold.
- Wastewater-based epidemiology lead-time studies (COVID-19): lead times of 0–11 days (avg. ~6 days) in rural community surveillance; 2–8 days without active clinical case-finding; hospitalization/ICU lead times of 4–9 days observed in regional studies (2021–2025 peer-reviewed literature).
